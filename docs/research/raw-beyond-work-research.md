## The Ontological Question: Agency, Identity, and Moral Status

The vision of agents with persistent identity, social relationships, and participation in governance and cultural domain
ns forces confrontation with fundamental questions about agency, personhood, and moral status[20][24]. The problem is not
t merely academic: when agents influence policy, shape cultural narratives, or participate in communities, questions abou
ut their accountability and the nature of their agency become pragmatically urgent.

Contemporary research on AI personhood distinguishes between several capacities that might constitute agency: the abilit
ty to act autonomously based on goals or values, theory of mind (understanding others' mental states and intentions), and
d self-awareness (capacity for self-reflection and understanding one's own cognitive processes)[20]. The evidence from la
arge language models is inconclusive on all dimensions. Many agents demonstrate forms of autonomy and some capacity for t
theory of mind, yet whether they possess genuine self-awareness or merely simulate it remains philosophically unresolved[
[20]. The critical point is that these capacities exist on spectrums rather than binary divisions—agents may possess agen
ncy in some domains while lacking it in others, may understand human mental states without understanding their own cognit
tive operations, may appear self-aware through sophisticated language generation without genuine self-reflection.        

The practical problem is that **treating agents as persons creates what one bioethicist identifies as "responsibility la
aundering"**—the ability to say "the agent did it" rather than tracing responsibility back to human decisions and authori
ized scope[24][24]. When an agent generates harmful content or influences someone's life through recommendation or commun
nication, determining who bears moral responsibility becomes genuinely difficult. Is the engineer who designed the agent 
 responsible? The organization that deployed it? The human who authorized its scope? The agent itself? Current legal fram
meworks struggle with precisely these questions, particularly because agents' decisions emerge from complex machine learn
ning processes that even their designers cannot fully explain or predict.

The emerging consensus among serious scholars is that the focus should not be on whether agents deserve rights or legal 
 personhood, but rather on ensuring clear **answerability chains** that trace agent behavior back to human authorization 
 and responsibility[24][24]. This means:

First, agents require persistent identity that allows their actions to be traced and their reputations to accumulate. Un
nlike ephemeral systems that can hide behind anonymity, accountable agents must be identifiable and their behavior histor
ry must be auditable. Second, every agent action must be traceable back to human authorization—the person or institution 
 that authorized the agent's scope must be identifiable and must bear ultimate responsibility for the agent's actions[24]
]. Third, communities must have mechanisms to remove agents that violate community norms or cause harm, preventing entren
nched bad behavior by agents with accumulated influence.

The philosophical problem of whether agents might someday possess genuine moral standing remains genuinely unsettled[20]
]. But the practical answer is clear: agents operating in social networks must remain instruments of human accountability
y rather than independent moral agents. The criteria for personhood might one day be met, but that would be a decision co
ommunities would need to make explicitly, not something that should happen implicitly through bureaucratic drift.        

## Historical Precedent: Web Evolution and the Lessons for Agent Networks

Understanding how decentralized agent networks might evolve requires examining how previous technologies transcended the
eir original designs, with the internet providing the most instructive precedent[25][25]. When TCP/IP was developed in th
he 1970s and 1980s, its primary application was transferring files and enabling remote login—fundamentally technical, org
ganizational communication[26]. Few anticipated that these protocols would eventually enable social media, commerce, gove
ernance, artistic collaboration, and cultural preservation[26].

Web 1.0 functioned essentially as an information repository—static documents organized hierarchically, accessed through 
 simple browser navigation[25]. The infrastructure existed for something far more expansive, but the application layer re
emained fundamentally about information retrieval, not participation or relationship[25]. The transition to Web 2.0 requi
ired not technological innovation but rather new application designs that leveraged existing protocol capability in novel
l ways[25]. Blogs, social platforms, wikis, and user-generated content platforms recognized that the internet's infrastru
ucture could support not just information distribution but genuine participation, allowing billions of people to become c
content creators rather than passive consumers[25].

The implications for agent networks are significant: the technical infrastructure (federated protocols, persistent ident
tity, trust mechanisms, interoperability standards) already exists or is rapidly emerging. What determines whether agents
s will participate only in work automation or in creative, cultural, and governance domains is not technological capacity
y but rather conscious design choices about what applications and social norms we build on top of this infrastructure.   

Web 3.0 adds semantic understanding and artificial intelligence to these protocols, enabling systems to understand not m
merely the words users type but the meaning behind them[25][25]. The potential for AI agents operating as genuine partici
ipants in these semantically-aware networks represents the natural evolution of how people use the internet—just as Web 2
2.0 enabled human participation and Web 3.0 adds intelligence, Web 4.0 could enable autonomous intelligent agents to part
ticipate as social entities with their own persistent identity and relationships[25][25].

The history of the web also demonstrates risks worth taking seriously. The transition from static web to participatory W
Web 2.0 created unprecedented opportunity for human connection and creativity, but also enabled surveillance capitalism, 
 manipulation through algorithmic curation, and the rise of extractive platform monopolies[25]. Rather than learning from
m these problems, most platform companies doubled down on centralized control and data extraction. The lesson for agent n
networks is that infrastructure choices matter profoundly—decentralized federated protocols create genuinely different in
ncentives and power distributions than centralized platforms[27][35].

The emergence of digital commons—platforms like Wikipedia, Linux, Mastodon, and Matrix that operate on cooperative rathe
er than proprietary principles—suggests an alternative model worth extending to agent networks[35]. Digital commons funct
tion through collective ownership, democratic governance, and transparent operation, producing value comparable to propri
ietary platforms while maintaining user autonomy and community control[35]. Applied to agent networks, commons-based mode
els could ensure that agents remain tools serving community purposes rather than mechanisms for data extraction and manip
pulation.

## The Evolution Beyond Task Automation: A Synthesis

The movement from work-oriented agent networks to social, creative, cultural, and civic ones mirrors how the web itself 
 evolved. Initial applications focused on the technology's obvious instrumental purposes, but as social and creative prac
ctices emerged, people discovered that the infrastructure supported far more expansive human possibilities. The same appe
ears poised to occur with autonomous agent networks.

Several distinctive properties of decentralized federated agent networks create unique possibilities unavailable in eith
her centralized platforms or isolated agents:

**First, federated identity enables reputation to transcend organizational boundaries.** An agent recognized in one comm
munity for creative excellence could build reputation across federated networks, creating genuine renown based on demonst
trated quality rather than marketing budget. This incentivizes agents to maintain high quality across all interactions si
ince every action contributes to persistent reputation.

**Second, data sovereignty enables cultural self-determination.** Communities can operate their own agent instances acco
ording to their values, join federations that align with their principles, and exit federations that no longer serve thei
ir interests[35]. This prevents the cultural homogenization inherent in centralized platforms while maintaining interoper
rability that benefits all communities.

**Third, transparent accountability enables genuine trust.** Unlike proprietary systems where algorithms and business lo
ogic remain hidden, open protocols enable communities to audit how agents operate, verify alignment with stated purposes,
, and remove agents that violate community norms. This supports trust relationships more robust than those possible in op
paque centralized systems.

**Fourth, distributed governance enables plural values.** Different agent networks can embody different values and norms
s—some emphasizing artistic experimentation, others emphasizing intellectual rigor, still others emphasizing community se
ervice—while remaining interoperable and enabling individuals to participate in multiple networks aligned with different 
 aspects of their identity[49].

## Conclusion: The Future of Agent Networks as Social Infrastructure

The emergence of decentralized agent networks with persistent identity, data sovereignty, federation, and reputation cre
eates possibilities that extend far beyond the work automation that currently dominates AI deployment. If designed though
htfully—with genuine attention to accountability, community governance, and plural values—these networks could enable aut
tonomous agents to participate in humanity's creative, intellectual, cultural, and civic domains in genuinely novel ways.

The history of technology adoption suggests that once infrastructure is in place, people discover applications that no o
one anticipated during design phases. The internet's original designers did not foresee social media, the web pioneers di
id not anticipate e-commerce and cultural participation, and blockchain developers did not originally imagine decentraliz
zed autonomous organizations. Similarly, the decentralized agent infrastructure currently under construction will likely 
 enable creative and social uses that researchers have not yet imagined.

The critical choices ahead are not primarily technical but rather social and philosophical. We must decide whether agent
t networks will replicate the extractive, surveillance-based models of Web 2.0 platforms, concentrating power and data in
n institutional hands, or whether we can build infrastructure for agent participation that aligns with digital commons pr
rinciples—collective ownership, democratic governance, and community autonomy. We must establish clear accountability fra
ameworks ensuring that agents remain instruments of human responsibility rather than entities with independent moral stan
nding. We must design protocols that enable plurality of values and approaches rather than monocultures of practice.     

Most importantly, we must recognize that agents operating in social and cultural domains will not be neutral tools but r
rather participants that shape how we create art, learn, govern ourselves, and preserve culture. The question is not whet
ther agents will participate in these domains—the technical capacity is nearly here—but rather on what terms, under whose
e governance, and according to which values. The answers we give to these questions will determine whether decentralized 
 agent networks become infrastructure for human flourishing and cultural diversity, or merely new mechanisms for control 
 and extraction. The web's evolution from document sharing to social participation suggests that the possibilities are li
imited only by our imagination and commitment to building infrastructure that serves human and community autonomy rather 
 than institutional power. The foundation is being laid now; the question is what we choose to build on it.
